Mastering Group Travel Requests

Group travel is the most complex — and often the most rewarding — work a Butler can do. When you get it right, you've earned the loyalty of an entire party at once.

A solo traveler texting a Butler is one conversation. A group of 18 colleagues traveling to Lisbon for a company offsite is eighteen conversations, eighteen preferences, and eighteen potential problems — all arriving at once. Butlers who master group travel requests open the door to some of the most lucrative and relationship-building work in the VELTM network.

What Counts as a Group?

At VELTM, a group request is generally any trip involving five or more travelers moving together with shared logistics. This includes:

  • Corporate team offsites and incentive trips
  • Destination weddings and celebration travel
  • Multi-generational family reunions
  • Friend groups celebrating milestones
  • Association and conference travel

Each has a different decision-making structure, a different emotional temperature, and a different definition of success. Understanding which type you're dealing with shapes every decision you make.

Room Blocks: The Foundation of Group Logistics

Room blocks are at the heart of most group accommodations. A block is a pre-negotiated set of rooms held by a hotel for a specific party, typically with a group rate and a release date after which unsold rooms return to general inventory.

  • Start early: Desirable properties in popular destinations fill up months in advance. For large groups (20+ rooms), initiate conversations at least six months out.
  • Negotiate beyond rate: Room upgrades for the trip organizer, complimentary meeting space, welcome amenities, and flexible attrition clauses are all on the table.
  • Manage the rooming list: Collect individual traveler names, special requests, and arrival/departure times systematically. A shared document or the VELTM platform's group tools are your best friend here.
  • Watch attrition: Most contracts require you to book a minimum percentage of the block. Track RSVPs carefully and communicate early if numbers change — hotels are generally willing to renegotiate before the deadline, not after.

Coordinating Multiple Travelers

The logistics of moving a large group from different origin cities to the same destination — and then managing them on the ground — require a level of orchestration that separates skilled Butlers from great ones.

  • Build a master itinerary: One document, shared with the organizer, listing every transfer, activity, meal, and check-in time. Update it in real time as changes occur.
  • Identify the decision-maker: In groups, there's always one person who holds the authority to approve changes. Find them early and communicate through them unless individual arrangements are specifically needed.
  • Account for different arrival windows: Group members rarely land at the same time. Coordinate a transport solution that handles staggered arrivals gracefully — whether that's a shuttle schedule or a pool of vehicles on standby.
  • Plan for the stragglers: Someone will always be late, miss a connection, or have a bag delayed. Have a contingency for common disruptions baked into the plan before departure day.

Group Dining: More Than Just a Big Table

Feeding a large group well is a skill in its own right. Restaurants have limits on how many covers they can serve at once, and a poorly organized group dinner can become a logistics nightmare.

  • Private dining rooms: For groups of 10 or more, request a private or semi-private space. It reduces noise, improves service, and allows for speeches or presentations if needed.
  • Set menus over a la carte: A pre-arranged set menu ensures consistent pacing and eliminates the chaos of 18 people ordering independently. Negotiate the menu in advance with the restaurant.
  • Collect dietary information early: Send a simple form to all group members well before arrival. Pass consolidated dietary requirements to the restaurant at least 48 hours before the reservation.
  • Arrange billing in advance: Agree on the payment structure before the meal — one master bill, split evenly, or individual tabs. Surprises at the end of a group dinner create bad memories.

Communication Tips for Large Parties

Communication is where group travel requests succeed or fall apart. You can have perfect logistics and still deliver a poor experience if travelers feel uninformed or ignored.

  • Create a group channel: With the organizer's permission, set up a single group thread (WhatsApp, Slack, or the VELTM platform) for broadcast updates. Keep individual queries separate.
  • Over-communicate the schedule: Send reminders 24 hours before each major logistics event (departure, check-in, group dinner). Assume that people haven't read the master itinerary.
  • Set clear response expectations: Let the organizer know your response window and how to reach you for urgent matters. A delayed response during a group move is felt by everyone at once.
  • Document every change: When something in a group itinerary changes, write it down, confirm it with the organizer, and broadcast the update to affected travelers. Verbal-only changes get lost.

The Payoff

Group travel requests require more upfront effort, but the relationship rewards are disproportionate. Deliver a smooth corporate offsite and you may earn a contract to handle every trip that company books. Nail a destination wedding and you'll receive referrals from every guest who attended.
